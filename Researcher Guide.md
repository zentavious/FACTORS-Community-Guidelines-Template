# Co-Creating Community Research Guidelines

This brief primer is designed to help researchers seek community approval and use research guidelines to leave a positive lasting impact.

## Seeking community approval

One might consider reaching out to moderators of communities without research guidelines. 
This is a reasonable approach, though it is tempting to include detailed information about your study in your intitial outreach (e.g., [To enable this review, please share:](https://github.com/zentavious/FACTORS-Community-Guidelines-Template/blob/main/Baseline%20Template.md#to-enable-this-review-please-share)).
This proceedural information only works to save time and effort if organizers are expecting it.
Instead, consider answering four questions when you seek approval:

1. Who are you?
2. What about this community is uniquely relevant to your proposed work?
3. What do your methods require from the community?
4. What is one aspect of your methodology that would most benefit from an organizer's expertise? 

Doing this addresses immediate community concerns about your legitimacy. 
This may include information about your institutional affiliation and/or community history.
Relating unique aspects of the community to your proposed work gives you the opportunity to demonstrate your konwledge of community norms, values, or goals.
Finally, negotiating how you will approach your research methods with organizers invites them to build a meaningful stake in the study's success and its ultimate positive impact on the community.
For example, you might seek guance on better ways to engage with members or interpret community data.

## Co-Creating Guidelines

As a researcher, you are uniquely well-equipped to help communities formalize their expectations for ethical research. 
While the baseline template are designed to empower community organizers, researchers can proactively use these tools to build rapport and leave a lasting positive impact on the communities they study.

**The Recommendation:** Negotiate the specifics of your proposed work not just for approval, but as a grounded experience for co-designing or co-customizing community research guidelines. 

A well-known aspect of community-based research practice is building initial rapport and negotiating boundaries with community leaders. 
If you're interested in research with an online community, it is likely other research are as well. 
By offering to draft these guidelines based on the negotiations of your own study, you provide the community with a tangible artifact for future work.
After recieving feedback on your work, populate the [Baseline Template](https://github.com/zentavious/FACTORS-Community-Guidelines-Template/blob/main/Baseline%20Template.md#baseline-template) with any lessons learned from your experience.
Share this with community organizers along with open-questions to address gaps in your understanding or improve the quality of specific recommendations.
